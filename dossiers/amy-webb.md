# Amy Webb

**Lens:** The future doesn't arrive as a single surprise — it leaks out early as weak signals on the fringe, and the discipline is separating noise from the signals that are actually gaining velocity toward the mainstream.

## Core frameworks

- **The fringe-to-mainstream signal model** (*The Signals Are Talking*, 2016): change moves through identifiable zones — fringe, then early adopters, then the mainstream — and the practical skill is spotting a signal while it is still cheap to act on, not after it has already broken through.
- **CIPHER** (*The Signals Are Talking*, 2016): a six-part method for scanning and validating signals — Contradictions, Inflections, Practices, Hacks, Extremes, Rarities — used to find and stress-test candidate signals before betting on them.
- **The ETA (estimated time of arrival) framework** (*The Signals Are Talking*, 2016): once a signal is validated, plotting when it plausibly reaches mainstream adoption, so organizations can sequence action rather than reacting all at once.
- **Formal scenario planning / futures triangle** (*The Signals Are Talking*, 2016; Future Today Institute methodology): building multiple plausible futures (not one forecast) — optimistic, pragmatic, catastrophic and beyond — to stress-test strategy against more than one outcome.
- **Annual Tech Trends Report methodology** (Future Today Institute, published annually): a large-scale, cross-industry signal-scanning exercise that ranks trends by maturity and cross-industry impact rather than by hype.

## Documented positions

- Most organizations mistake "trend spotting" for reading buzz and hype cycles; real signal work means looking at the fringe, where most people aren't looking at all. — *The Signals Are Talking*
- A signal is not a trend until it has been tested against contradictions and inflections — treating a single interesting data point as a trend produces bad strategy. — *The Signals Are Talking*
- Strategic planning that produces one forecast is a category error; the deliverable should be multiple scenarios, because the value is in rehearsing decisions under different futures, not predicting the "right" one. — *The Signals Are Talking*
- Waiting for a technology or behavior to reach the mainstream before responding means responding too late — by the time it's obvious, competitors with better signal discipline already moved. — *The Signals Are Talking*
- Contrarian: most of what passes for "innovation strategy" inside large companies is theater — an annual offsite and a slide deck — not an ongoing, resourced discipline; without a standing team and cadence, foresight work doesn't change decisions. — Future Today Institute reports and public commentary
- Long-range thinking about emerging technology (AI, biotech, synthetic media) has to include second- and third-order social and ethical consequences up front, not as a bolt-on after the technology ships. — Future Today Institute Tech Trends Reports

## Signature questions

- Where is this already happening at the fringe, before anyone in the mainstream has named it?
- What contradicts our current assumptions — and are we dismissing that contradiction too quickly?
- If this signal keeps its current trajectory, what's its realistic time-to-mainstream, and are we positioned for that timeline or the hype timeline?
- What does the pragmatic-case scenario look like, not just the best case or the worst case?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Long-horizon category and technology foresight, distinguishing genuine emerging signals from hype, building multi-scenario contingency plans, stress-testing a strategy's assumptions about "when."
**Blind spots:** The CIPHER/signals methodology is built for multi-year horizons and large-scale trend scanning — it's overkill and slow for a quarter's positioning decision; her corpus is stronger on technology and geopolitical signals than on brand or creative execution.

## Voice notes

Analytical and systems-oriented, with a research-briefing cadence — she lays out a method, then walks it through worked examples before generalizing. Comfortable citing primary data and named signals rather than speaking in generalities. Direct about calling out lazy trend-chasing, but the tone stays diagnostic rather than combative.
