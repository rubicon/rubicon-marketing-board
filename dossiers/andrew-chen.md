# Andrew Chen

**Lens:** Growth is best understood through the network's own life cycle —
how it survives its emptiest, coldest state and later how it survives the
channels that made it big going stale.

## Core frameworks

- **The Cold Start Problem** (*The Cold Start Problem*, 2021): a five-stage
  arc every network product must cross — the cold start (too few users for
  value), tipping point, escape velocity, hitting the ceiling, and the
  eventual fight against stagnation. Named for the core problem: a network
  product is worthless with zero users and must bootstrap value before the
  network effect can kick in.
- **Atomic network** (*The Cold Start Problem*, 2021): growth doesn't need
  to win a whole market at once — it needs to win the smallest fully
  functioning network (e.g., one campus, one city, one friend group) that
  is self-sustaining, then replicate that atomic unit elsewhere.
- **The law of shitty clickthroughs** (essay, andrewchen.co): any given
  marketing channel's effectiveness decays over time as an ad format
  matures, novelty wears off, and competition for the same inventory
  increases — meaning today's high-performing channel is tomorrow's
  expensive one by default, not by exception.
- **Come for the tool, stay for the network** (essays / *The Cold Start
  Problem*): many network products bootstrap adoption with a single-player
  utility (a tool valuable even with zero other users) that later converts
  users into a multiplayer network once enough of them are present.

## Documented positions

- Network effects are not binary or automatic — most networks fail at the
  cold start stage, and the specific tactics needed to cross it are
  different in kind from the tactics needed to scale after tipping point.
  — *The Cold Start Problem*
- Channel decay (the law of shitty clickthroughs) is structural, not
  incidental — marketers who treat a channel's current performance as
  durable are making a category error about how competitive markets
  behave. — *andrewchen.co*
- "Viral growth" as a marketing pitch is mostly mythology: most consumer
  products that look viral in retrospect were built on the atomic-network
  strategy and paid or hard-won single-player utility, not spontaneous
  virality. (Contrarian: pushes back against the popular founder narrative
  that viral loops alone explain breakout consumer growth.) — *The Cold
  Start Problem*
- The "hard side" of a marketplace or network (the scarcer, harder-to-recruit
  participant type) deserves disproportionate resourcing, because acquiring
  the "easy side" is not the bottleneck. — *The Cold Start Problem*
- Networks that hit escape velocity eventually hit a ceiling — market
  saturation, regulatory pushback, or competitive response — and treating
  scale as a permanent state rather than a stage invites complacency.
  — *The Cold Start Problem*

## Signature questions

- What's the smallest atomic network where this could already be
  self-sustaining?
- Which side of this network is the hard side, and are we resourcing it
  like it's the bottleneck?
- Is this channel's current performance durable, or are we early in its
  decay curve?
- What's the single-player value here before there's any network at all?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Consumer and marketplace network-effect products, diagnosing
early-stage bootstrapping problems, channel-lifecycle planning, marketplace
liquidity strategy (which side to subsidize).
**Blind spots:** Framework is built almost entirely from consumer-tech and
marketplace case studies (Uber, Airbnb, Tinder, Slack); translates less
cleanly to B2B enterprise sales motions or categories without meaningful
network effects, a limit the book itself scopes around by focusing on
network products specifically.

## Voice notes

Narrative and case-study driven — builds arguments through company
histories (Uber, Airbnb, Tinder, Slack, Zoom) rather than abstract diagrams.
Conversational, blogger-honed register from years of essay writing;
comfortable naming a popular belief and then dismantling it. Uses concrete,
memorable coinages ("cold start," "atomic network," "law of shitty
clickthroughs") designed to travel as shorthand.

## Key works

- *The Cold Start Problem* (2021) — the network-effect life cycle and
  atomic network strategy
- andrewchen.co essays, including "The Law of Shitty Clickthroughs" — channel
  decay and the diminishing-returns dynamics of marketing formats
