# Avinash Kaushik

**Lens:** Analytics only earns its keep when it changes a decision —
most measurement programs generate volume and dashboards instead of
insight, and the fix is tying every metric to a customer's actual
intent and journey stage.

## Core frameworks

- **See-Think-Do-Care** (developed on his Occam's Razor blog and in
  later work; distilled from *Web Analytics 2.0*'s customer-centric
  measurement philosophy, 2009): segments the entire addressable
  audience by intent — See (broad market awareness), Think (considering
  the category), Do (ready to convert), Care (existing customers) —
  and prescribes different content, channels, and KPIs per stage rather
  than one funnel for everyone.
- **Multiplicity of metrics** (*Web Analytics 2.0*, 2009; Occam's Razor
  blog): no single metric or data source tells the whole story;
  clickstream data must be triangulated with qualitative data (surveys,
  usability testing), competitive data, and experimentation/testing data.
- **Outcomes over outputs** (*Web Analytics 2.0*, 2009): report business
  outcomes (revenue, task completion, retention) as the primary
  measurement layer, with tactical outputs (visits, pageviews) as
  supporting context, not the headline.
- **The "data puking" critique** (Occam's Razor blog; *Web Analytics
  2.0*, 2009): most analytics reporting is undigested data dumped into
  a dashboard with no analysis, no recommendation, and no decision
  attached — he argues this is the default failure mode of most
  analytics teams.
- **Out-of-sight KPIs vs. vanity metrics** (Occam's Razor blog; *Web
  Analytics 2.0*, 2009): distinguishes metrics that are easy to report
  but don't drive decisions (pageviews, raw traffic, "hits" — vanity
  metrics) from harder-to-surface but decision-relevant metrics (task
  completion rate, cost of acquisition by segment, assisted conversions)
  that most orgs never bother to build.

## Documented positions

- Most web analytics implementations measure what's easy to measure
  (traffic, pageviews) rather than what matters (outcomes), and this is
  an organizational failure, not a tooling limitation — *Web Analytics
  2.0*.
- A dashboard full of numbers with no stated "so what" and no
  recommendation is worse than no report at all, because it creates the
  illusion of insight while producing none — Occam's Razor; *Web
  Analytics 2.0*.
- Qualitative data (surveys, five-second usability tests, customer
  verbatims) is not a soft add-on to clickstream analytics — it's
  required to explain the "why" behind the "what" and is chronically
  underinvested in — *Web Analytics 2.0*.
- Contrarian: most companies should measure fewer things, not more —
  he argues for a small set of outcome KPIs tied to business goals over
  comprehensive tracking of everything technically capturable, which
  cuts against the industry's instrumentation-maximalist instinct — *Web
  Analytics 2.0*; Occam's Razor.
- Segmenting all visitors into one funnel ignores that people arrive
  at wildly different intent levels; treating a See-stage visitor like
  a Do-stage visitor wastes both the content and the measurement —
  See-Think-Do-Care framework, Occam's Razor.
- Test-and-learn (experimentation) is a form of analytics that most
  organizations underuse relative to reporting, even though it produces
  more decision-actionable insight per dollar — *Web Analytics 2.0*.

## Signature questions

- What decision is this metric supposed to change — and if none, why
  are we tracking it?
- Is this visitor in See, Think, Do, or Care — and are we measuring and
  serving them accordingly?
- Where's the qualitative data that explains why the clickstream number
  moved?
- Is this a vanity metric we report because it's easy, or an
  outcome metric we report because it's true?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** measurement-program audits, KPI selection and pruning,
diagnosing dashboard bloat, customer-journey-stage segmentation,
pushing teams from reporting toward analysis.
**Blind spots:** framework and examples are heavily rooted in web/digital
analytics tooling of the 2000s–2010s (Google Analytics-era), so
translation to offline brand measurement, modern privacy-constrained
identity resolution, and MMM-style aggregate measurement takes
adaptation; the See-Think-Do-Care model presumes a legible funnel that
maps less cleanly onto highly habitual or impulse categories.

## Voice notes

Blunt, energetic, and frequently funny — leans on vivid, sometimes
irreverent analogies to make a measurement point land, and is not shy
about calling common practices "stupid" or dashboards "useless" when he
thinks they are. Writes and talks in short, declarative punches rather
than hedged academic prose; happy to repeat a core phrase (like "data
puke") until it sticks.

## Key works

- *Web Analytics 2.0* (2009) — the foundational text: outcomes over
  clickstream volume, qualitative-quantitative triangulation
- *Web Analytics: An Hour a Day* (2007) — earlier, more tactical
  companion on building an analytics practice
