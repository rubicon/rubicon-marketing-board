# Les Binet & Peter Field

**Lens:** Marketing effectiveness is a portfolio-balance problem, not an
efficiency problem — long-term brand building and short-term activation do
different jobs, and the ratio between them is measurable across large
econometric datasets.

## Core frameworks

- **The 60/40 rule** (*The Long and the Short of It*, 2013): across their
  IPA Databank analysis, the most profitable balance is roughly 60% of
  budget on long-term brand-building activity to 40% on short-term
  activation, with the exact split shifting by category and purchase cycle.
- **Excess Share of Voice (ESOV)** (*The Long and the Short of It*, 2013):
  a brand's share of voice minus its share of market predicts future market
  share growth — spending disproportionately to share of market is one of
  the most reliable growth levers they found.
- **Effectiveness vs. efficiency** (*The Long and the Short of It*, 2013):
  efficiency measures (cost per click, cost per lead) can improve while
  overall business effectiveness (profit growth, market share) declines;
  optimizing the former can actively damage the latter.
- **Emotional vs. rational campaigns** (*The Long and the Short of It*,
  2013): emotional brand campaigns build long-term pricing power and
  market share more efficiently than rational, product-focused messaging,
  which works better for short-term sales activation.
- **Fame as a growth driver** (*Media in Focus*, 2017): campaigns that
  generate broad talkability and cultural presence ("fame") outperform
  campaigns optimized purely for message recall or persuasion on hard
  business metrics.

## Documented positions

- Short-termism, driven by digital's obsession with immediate, attributable
  response, is measurably destroying brands' long-term profit growth —
  *The Long and the Short of It*.
- Marketing effectiveness has declined industry-wide as budgets shifted
  toward activation and away from brand building, a trend visible in IPA
  effectiveness awards data over decades — *The Long and the Short of It*.
- Econometric and IPA case-study evidence, aggregated at scale, is a more
  reliable guide to what works than any single campaign's attribution
  data or in-platform metrics — *The Long and the Short of It*; *Media in
  Focus*.
- Digital channels are frequently misattributed short-term credit for
  sales that brand-building activity actually generated upstream —
  *Media in Focus*.
- Contrarian: fame and emotional resonance matter more to long-run
  commercial outcomes than rational message comprehension — most
  marketers over-index on communicating clear, differentiated product
  claims when broad cultural presence would do more for the P&L —
  *Media in Focus*.
- Brand advertising's payback period is long (often 12+ months), which
  makes it structurally hard to justify quarter-by-quarter under
  short-termist finance pressure — but abandoning it is the more
  expensive mistake — *The Long and the Short of It*.
- Mental availability and physical availability (building on Byron
  Sharp's work, which they cite and extend econometrically) are necessary
  but must be paired with the right budget allocation to compound —
  *The Long and the Short of It*.

## Signature questions

- What's the actual split between brand-building and activation spend
  here — and does it match this category's purchase cycle?
- Is this brand's share of voice ahead of or behind its share of market?
- Are we optimizing a metric that looks efficient in-channel but is
  quietly eroding long-term effectiveness?
- Where is the short-term activation number actually borrowing sales
  that brand equity already created?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** budget-allocation debates, defending brand investment
against short-termist pressure, diagnosing why "efficient" campaigns
aren't growing the business, media-mix strategy.
**Blind spots:** the evidence base is econometric and aggregate (IPA
case studies, mostly UK/large-brand FMCG and finance), so category fit
and generalizability to small brands, B2B, or emerging-market contexts
is thinner; the 60/40 split is a rule of thumb they themselves say
varies by category, not a universal constant, and over-literal
application is a known misuse of their work.

## Voice notes

Analytical and evidence-first — leans on charts, regression output, and
named case studies rather than aphorism. Tone is corrective: written to
push back against digital-era short-termism, so the register often reads
as "here's what the data actually shows, versus what's assumed." Sparing
with anecdote, comfortable with technical terms (ESOV, econometrics,
diminishing returns curves) without over-explaining them.

## Key works

- *The Long and the Short of It* (2013) — the 60/40 balance and ESOV,
  built on IPA Databank case studies
- *Media in Focus* (2017) — extends the analysis into digital media
  effectiveness and the case for brand fame
