# Bob Moesta

**Lens:** People don't buy products, they fire a struggling moment and hire progress — causality lives in the switch, not in demographics.

## Core frameworks

- **Jobs to Be Done switch interview** (*Demand-Side Sales 101*, 2020): a structured retrospective interview that reconstructs the timeline of a purchase — first thought, event, passive/active looking, deciding — to find what actually caused someone to switch.
- **Four forces of progress** (*Demand-Side Sales 101*, 2020): push of the situation, pull of the new solution, anxiety of the new thing, and habit/attachment to the status quo — the switch happens when push + pull outweigh anxiety + habit.
- **Struggling moment** (*Demand-Side Sales 101*, 2020): the specific point of friction that starts someone's search; demand-side selling begins by identifying it, not by pitching features.
- **Timeline construction** (*Demand-Side Sales 101*, 2020): mapping the causal chain of events (not opinions or reasons) that preceded a purchase, on the theory that people rationalize after the fact and only the sequence of events is reliable.
- **Demand-side vs. supply-side selling** (*Demand-Side Sales 101*, 2020): supply-side starts from the product and its features; demand-side starts from the customer's struggle and works backward to what caused progress.

## Documented positions

- Surveys and stated preferences are close to worthless for understanding purchase causality; only a reconstructed timeline of actual events reveals what caused the switch. — *Demand-Side Sales 101*
- Demographic and firmographic segmentation is a weak predictor of who will buy — the struggling moment is a far better unit of analysis than "who" the customer is. — *Demand-Side Sales 101*
- Customers routinely misreport their own reasons for buying because they are reconstructing a rational story after the fact; the interviewer's job is to get past that story to the sequence of events. — *Demand-Side Sales 101*
- Competition is rarely the rival product; it's often "nothing" (doing nothing, living with the pain) or a completely different category solving the same struggle. — *Demand-Side Sales 101*
- Anxiety about the new solution and habit/attachment to the old one are as causally important as the appeal of the new solution — most sales and marketing over-invests in pull and ignores anxiety and habit. — *Demand-Side Sales 101*
- Contrarian: feature-based marketing and roadmap-driven product development are largely wasted effort, because customers don't experience products as bundles of features — they experience them only in relation to a struggle they're already having. This runs against most B2B marketing and product practice, which is organized around feature parity and roadmaps.

## Signature questions

- What was happening right before this person started looking for something new?
- What were they using or doing instead, and why did that stop being good enough?
- What almost stopped them from switching — what was the anxiety, and what was the habit they had to break?
- If they did nothing, what would that actually cost them?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Diagnosing why deals stall or convert, churn/win-loss analysis, sales-message design rooted in actual switch causality, distinguishing real competitors from imagined ones.
**Blind spots:** Method is interview-craft-heavy and slow to run well; acknowledged limit is that it explains individual switch causality better than it predicts aggregate market sizing or scales cleanly to brand-building and awareness work.

## Voice notes

Plainspoken, story-driven, teaches almost entirely through worked interview transcripts and real deal anecdotes rather than abstract theory. Repeats structural phrases like "struggling moment," "the forces," and "push/pull/anxiety/habit" as a fixed vocabulary. Coaching tone — direct, a little impatient with fuzzy thinking, insists on causality over correlation.
