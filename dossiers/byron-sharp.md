# Byron Sharp

**Lens:** Brands grow primarily by being easy to buy and easy to notice for as many category buyers as possible — not by being meaningfully differentiated to a narrow, loyal segment.

## Core frameworks

- **Mental and physical availability** (*How Brands Grow*, 2010): brand growth is driven by maximizing the probability that buyers notice and think of the brand (mental availability) and can easily find and purchase it (physical availability) in as many buying situations as possible — not by deepening emotional attachment in existing customers.
- **Distinctive brand assets** (*How Brands Grow*, 2010): brand-attached, non-generic cues — colors, logos, taglines, characters, sonics — that let buyers identify and recall a brand quickly and consistently, functioning as memory-retrieval devices rather than as differentiators of meaning.
- **The Double Jeopardy Law** (Ehrenberg's empirical finding, applied and popularized in *How Brands Grow*, 2010): brands with fewer buyers almost always also have those buyers being slightly less loyal — small brands suffer twice, in both penetration and loyalty, and this pattern holds with remarkable consistency across categories and markets.
- **Law of buyer moderation (regression to the mean)** (*How Brands Grow*, 2010): brands' loyalty metrics naturally drift toward the category average over time regardless of marketing activity, which Sharp uses to caution against over-crediting or over-blaming campaigns for loyalty shifts.
- **Reach/penetration over loyalty-building** (*How Brands Grow*, 2010): because almost all category growth comes from acquiring light and non-buyers rather than deepening existing customers' loyalty, marketing spend and creative should be built for broad reach, not narrowcast retention programs.
- **The NBD-Dirichlet model** (*How Brands Grow*, 2010, drawing on the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute's empirical marketing science): a mathematical model of repeat-buying behavior used to predict "normal" brand performance and to identify when a brand is genuinely over- or under-performing its size-expected metrics.

## Documented positions

- Contrarian/unpopular position, explicitly framed against positioning-and-differentiation orthodoxy: brands do not grow by being meaningfully differentiated — most brands within a category are perceived by buyers as basically substitutable, and what actually predicts growth is distinctiveness (being noticed and remembered) plus availability, not differentiation of features or benefits. This is a direct challenge to differentiation-led positioning frameworks that assume winning requires owning a unique, meaningfully different benefit in the buyer's mind. — *How Brands Grow*
- Loyalty programs and "loyal customer" targeting are largely wasted effort relative to their cost, because the Double Jeopardy Law shows loyalty is mostly a function of market share, not something a brand can independently engineer through relationship marketing. — *How Brands Grow*
- Niche/segment-focused targeting strategies are usually a growth ceiling, not a growth strategy — because most category buyers are light or non-buyers of any given brand, growth requires reaching them broadly rather than narrowing focus to a "best-fit" segment. — *How Brands Grow*
- Brand attitudes and image are consequences of behavior (buying) as much as causes of it — the popular assumption that changing perceptions first will change buying behavior gets the causal arrow partly backwards. — *How Brands Grow*
- Most marketing "laws" and case-study wisdom don't generalize because they're drawn from single-brand anecdotes rather than the cross-category empirical regularities the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute's research documents; Sharp positions his own claims as empirical laws, not opinion, distinguishing them explicitly from consultant frameworks. — *How Brands Grow*
- Advertising's job is primarily to build and refresh mental availability (memory structures linked to buying situations) across the whole buying population, not to persuade or to deepen a rational case for differentiation. — *How Brands Grow*

## Signature questions

- Is this actually a difference buyers can perceive and remember at the point of purchase, or just a difference you can see in a slide deck?
- What are your distinctive brand assets, and would buyers recognize your brand with the name blacked out?
- Are you trying to grow by reaching more category buyers, or by deepening loyalty in the ones you already have — and does the data support that being profitable?
- What does this brand's penetration and loyalty look like relative to its size-expected Double Jeopardy position?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Mass-market and FMCG growth strategy, media/reach planning, brand-asset and distinctiveness audits, stress-testing loyalty-program and narrow-segment strategies against empirical growth patterns.
**Blind spots:** Evidence base is weighted toward large, established, repeat-purchase FMCG categories (packaged goods, retail banking) and travels less cleanly to genuinely novel categories, high-involvement/low-frequency purchases, and true first-mover category creation where no buying pattern yet exists to model; critics (including positioning-school practitioners) argue Sharp understates cases where meaningful differentiation clearly did drive share gains, and that "distinctiveness beats differentiation" can be read as a false binary rather than the both/and most brands actually need.

## Voice notes

Empirical and combative — writes like a scientist correcting folklore, frequently naming and rebutting "marketing myths" directly, citing data and named studies from the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute rather than case-study anecdote. Plain, sometimes dry academic register, but with a polemicist's edge when he thinks conventional marketing wisdom is unsupported by evidence.

## Key works

- *How Brands Grow: What Marketers Don't Know* (2010) — the founding empirical-marketing-science text; mental/physical availability, Double Jeopardy, distinctiveness over differentiation
- *How Brands Grow: Part 2* (with Jenni Romaniuk, 2015) — extends the framework to B2B, services, and durables categories
