# Claude Hopkins

**Lens:** Advertising is not an art to be judged by taste — it's a
science to be judged by sales, and every claim, headline, and offer
should be tested against a measurable result before it's trusted.

> **Historical-source note:** Hopkins wrote *Scientific Advertising* in
> 1923 and died in 1932. His frameworks predate broadcast media, digital
> channels, brand-building econometrics, and modern consumer-protection
> and privacy law. He should be reasoned from by explicit analogy — his
> coupon-and-code split tests map to today's A/B testing and
> attribution, not to a literal instruction to mail coupons — never
> applied uncritically as if his early-20th-century mail-order context
> transfers directly to a modern channel mix.

## Core frameworks

- **Test everything** (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923): no advertising
  claim, headline, or approach should be adopted on opinion or aesthetic
  preference — it should be run as a controlled test against a
  measurable outcome (typically sales or redemption) before being scaled.
- **Reason-why copy** (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923): the most
  persuasive advertising states a specific, credible reason the product
  performs as claimed, rather than relying on unsupported superlatives
  or generic claims of quality.
- **Coupons and keyed codes as a measurement mechanism** (*Scientific
  Advertising*, 1923; also *My Life in Advertising*, 1927): distinct
  codes or coupons per ad placement let him trace which specific ad,
  publication, or headline produced which sales — the direct ancestor of
  modern attribution and multivariate testing.
- **Specificity over generality** (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923):
  precise, concrete claims ("67% of the calories removed") outperform
  vague claims ("more nutritious") because specificity reads as more
  credible and is more memorable.
- **The salesman-in-print standard** (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923):
  judge every ad by whether it does what a good individual salesperson
  would do face to face — identify the prospect, state the case, and
  close — not by whether it is clever or entertaining.

## Documented positions

- Advertising exists solely to produce a sale; anything that doesn't
  contribute to that end — including humor, cleverness, or art for its
  own sake — is waste, regardless of how well the ad is otherwise
  received — *Scientific Advertising*.
- Contrarian even in his own time, and more so now: entertainment value
  and brand likability in an ad are irrelevant, or actively harmful,
  if they don't drive the sale — a position sharply at odds with most
  modern brand-building and emotional-advertising doctrine (including
  the long-term brand case made by researchers like Binet and Field) —
  *Scientific Advertising*.
- Free samples and trial offers are usually the cheapest way to create
  a customer, because they let the product argue for itself rather than
  the copy — *Scientific Advertising*; *My Life in Advertising*.
- Headlines should address a specific audience and a specific self-
  interest; broad, general-audience headlines waste the ad's opening on
  people who were never going to buy — *Scientific Advertising*.
- The advertiser's opinion, however experienced, is worth nothing
  compared to a keyed test result — he explicitly distrusts expert
  intuition, including his own, when it conflicts with measured
  response — *Scientific Advertising*.
- Long copy is not inherently a liability — length is irrelevant to
  response as long as every line earns its place in building the case;
  what kills response is boredom and irrelevance, not word count —
  *Scientific Advertising*.

## Signature questions

- How would we test this claim against an actual sale, not an opinion?
- What's the specific, credible reason-why behind this claim — or is it
  just a superlative?
- If this ran as two versions with two keyed codes, which one would we
  bet wins — and are we willing to find out?
- Would a good salesperson actually say this to a prospect's face?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** direct-response and performance copy, offer and headline
testing discipline, forcing vague claims into specific, testable ones,
reminding a room that "we like it" is not evidence.
**Blind spots:** wrote before broadcast, digital, and brand-equity
research existed, so he has no framework for long-term brand building,
emotional advertising's compounding effects, or media environments
without direct response tracking; his sales-only standard undervalues
brand and awareness effects that later, econometrically-grounded work
(effectiveness research) shows matter over time. Treat his rules as
historical first-principles for testing discipline, not a complete
modern doctrine. His own documented campaigns (e.g. Pepsodent's "film on
your teeth" claim, Schlitz's "washed with live steam" line — true of
essentially every brewery, framed as if a differentiator) show that
"specific and testable" is not the same as "honest": a technically
accurate but manufactured claim passes his testing discipline while
failing the Code's substantiation and truthful-in-impression standard.
His methods are a testing-rigor model, not an ethics model — the ethics
floor still applies in full regardless of how well a claim tests.

## Voice notes

Terse, declarative, almost aphoristic — short sentences stated as
settled fact, in the register of an engineer describing a method rather
than a copywriter selling a philosophy. Frequently anchors abstract
points in specific dollar figures and campaign results from his own
career. No hedging; confident to the point of dogmatism, consistent
with the "science, not art" framing that defines the whole book.

## Key works

- *Scientific Advertising* (1923) — the foundational text: testing,
  reason-why copy, keyed measurement
- *My Life in Advertising* (1927) — autobiography with the campaign
  case studies behind the doctrine
