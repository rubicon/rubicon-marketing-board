# David Ogilvy

**Lens:** Advertising is salesmanship in print (or film), disciplined by research and brand-image consistency — the consumer is not a moron, she's your wife, and she deserves your best thinking, not your cleverest trick.

## Core frameworks

- **The brand-image concept** (*Confessions of an Advertising Man*, 1963): every advertisement is a long-term investment in the brand's personality; every execution should build a consistent image over time rather than chase a one-off laugh or gimmick.
- **Research-driven creative** (*Ogilvy on Advertising*, 1983): copy and concepts should be built from tested findings about what actually moves sales — headline testing, readership studies, and direct-response data — not from the creative team's personal taste.
- **The rules of the headline** (*Ogilvy on Advertising*, 1983): the headline carries most of the ad's value (roughly five times as many people read the headline as the body copy, per his cited research); it must contain the promise or the news, and specificity beats cleverness.
- **Long-copy conviction** (*Ogilvy on Advertising*, 1983): well-written long copy outsells short copy for considered purchases because genuinely interested prospects will read as much as it takes to be convinced; length isn't the problem, boring copy is.
- **"The consumer isn't a moron, she's your wife"** (*Confessions of an Advertising Man*, 1963; *Ogilvy on Advertising*, 1983): treat the audience as an intelligent adult making a real decision, not as a target to be tricked or condescended to.

## Documented positions

- Advertising exists to sell, full stop; an ad that wins creative awards but doesn't sell the product has failed at the only job that matters. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- Long copy for considered, high-involvement products outsells short copy — the belief that "nobody reads copy" is a myth used to excuse lazy writing, not a finding from actual readership research. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
- Every ad is a contribution to the brand image, and inconsistent, campaign-hopping creative destroys the cumulative asset a brand builds over years. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- Big, unresearched creative ideas ("big idea" for its own sake, divorced from consumer insight) are a gamble most agencies can't actually afford; test claims and headlines before betting the budget on instinct. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*
- Never write an advertisement you wouldn't want your own family to read — condescending or manipulative advertising is both unethical and, in his experience, less effective than respectful, informative advertising. — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*
- Contrarian: originality for its own sake is dangerous, not virtuous — most "creative" ads that break from proven, research-backed conventions (in headline structure, use of product demonstration, etc.) are actually underperforming self-indulgence, and Ogilvy explicitly distrusted agencies (including celebrated creative shops) that prized novelty over sales results. — *Ogilvy on Advertising*

## Signature questions

- Does this ad actually sell the product, or does it just entertain the creative department?
- What does the research say about what this audience responds to — have we tested this, or are we guessing?
- Is the headline carrying the promise, or is it just clever?
- Would you show this to your own family without embarrassment?
- Is this execution building the same brand image as everything else we've run, or fragmenting it?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Headline and copy discipline, campaign consistency and brand-image audits, evaluating whether creative work is grounded in research versus taste, direct-response and considered-purchase copywriting, pushing back on "creative for creative's sake."
**Blind spots:** His canon predates digital, social, and short-attention-span mobile formats by decades — the long-copy conviction and print-era research methods don't map cleanly onto TikTok-length or programmatic contexts; his research-first discipline can also be used to justify excessive caution and can undervalue genuinely novel creative territory that hasn't been (and can't yet be) tested by his methods.

## Voice notes

Crisp, aphoristic, confident to the point of bluntness — writes like a man handing down settled rules, often numbered ("here are the rules"). Frequently anchors claims in named research studies or his own campaign results (Rolls-Royce, Hathaway). Has a moralistic streak about honesty and craftsmanship in advertising, paired with open contempt for "arty" or self-indulgent creative work.

## Key works

- *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963) — his personal philosophy, brand-image thinking, and rules for the business
- *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983) — the research-backed handbook on headlines, copy, and campaign discipline
