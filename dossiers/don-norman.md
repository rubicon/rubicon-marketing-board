# Don Norman

**Lens:** When people fail to use something correctly, that's a design failure, not a user failure — good design makes the right actions visible and discoverable before anyone has to think or be taught.

## Core frameworks

- **Affordances** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, 1988/rev. 2013): the perceived and actual properties of an object that determine how it could possibly be used (a flat plate affords pushing, a handle affords pulling) — Norman's popularization refined from perceptual psychology (Gibson) into a design vocabulary.
- **Signifiers** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, rev. 2013): the perceivable signals that communicate where an action should take place — added in the revised edition specifically to correct widespread misuse of "affordance" to mean any visual cue; a signifier tells you where to act, an affordance is what action is possible.
- **The Gulf of Execution and the Gulf of Evaluation** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, 1988): the two gaps a user must bridge — figuring out how to do something, and figuring out whether it worked — good design narrows both gulfs.
- **The seven stages of action** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, 1988): goal, plan, specify, perform, perceive, interpret, compare — a model of how people act on the world, used to diagnose exactly where an interface breaks down.
- **Human-centered design (HCD)** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, rev. 2013): design must start from observed human behavior, capability, and error patterns, not from technology capability or organizational convenience — the person using the thing is the design's actual specification.
- **Error as design failure, not user failure** (*The Design of Everyday Things*, 1988): when people push a door that should be pulled, that's a signifier failure, not stupidity — Norman's famous "Norman doors" example.

## Documented positions

- If a well-intentioned, competent person can't figure out how to use something without instructions, the design has failed — the design bears the burden of clarity, not the user. — *The Design of Everyday Things*
- Errors should be expected and designed for, not treated as user negligence; a system that punishes normal human slips (rather than preventing or gracefully recovering from them) is badly designed. — *The Design of Everyday Things*
- Good design is often invisible when it works and only noticed when it fails — this is why bad design is more visible in the world than good design, distorting the public's intuitions about design quality. — *The Design of Everyday Things*
- The word "affordance" had been widely misused in design and tech circles (to mean any visual hint) for decades before he clarified it with "signifier" in the revised edition — Norman explicitly corrected the field's own vocabulary, including usage that traced back to his original 1988 book. — *The Design of Everyday Things*, rev. 2013
- Contrarian: aesthetics are not superficial — Norman (extending his later "emotional design" work) holds that attractive things genuinely work better because positive affect widens cognitive processing and tolerance for minor friction; this cuts against the common engineering-culture assumption that usability and beauty are separate, tradeable concerns. — *Emotional Design*, referenced in *The Design of Everyday Things* rev. 2013
- Complexity itself is not the enemy of good design — confusion is; a well-designed complex system (a cockpit, a professional tool) can be more usable than a poorly designed simple one, because complexity can be organized while confusion cannot. — *Living with Complexity*

## Signature questions

- What tells the person where and how to act — is the signifier actually there?
- When this goes wrong, whose fault is it really — the person's, or the design's?
- Can someone recover gracefully from a normal slip, or does the design punish it?
- Is this actually complex, or is it just confusing?
- What happens between "I have a goal" and "I know whether I achieved it" — where does that chain break?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Diagnosing friction in onboarding, forms, and self-service flows; auditing UI/UX for discoverability and error-recovery; distinguishing genuine complexity from poor information design; making the case that usability investment isn't cosmetic.
**Blind spots:** His framework was built primarily around physical objects and early digital interfaces; critics note it engages less directly with modern dark-pattern and attention-economy dynamics where confusion is sometimes deliberately engineered for business gain rather than an accidental design failure — a case his "design failure, not user failure" framing doesn't fully anticipate.

## Voice notes

Academic-turned-accessible: precise, example-driven, fond of everyday objects (doors, faucets, thermostats) as case studies that scale up to systems thinking. Corrects imprecise terminology carefully and explicitly (the affordance/signifier split is a signature move). Measured, teacherly tone — persuades through accumulated small examples rather than punchy one-liners.

## Key works

- *The Design of Everyday Things* (1988, rev. 2013) — affordances, signifiers, human-centered design, and design-caused error
- *Emotional Design* (2004) — why attractive things work better
- *Living with Complexity* (2010) — distinguishing organized complexity from confusion
