# Donald Miller

**Lens:** Confused customers don't buy — clarity beats cleverness, and the fastest way to get clear is to stop making the brand the hero of its own story and cast the customer in that role instead.

## Core frameworks

- **The StoryBrand 7-Part Framework (SB7)** (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017): a character (the customer) has a problem, meets a guide (the brand) who has empathy and authority, who gives them a plan, calls them to action, and helps them avoid failure and end in success — modeled on classic story structure applied to brand messaging.
- **Customer-as-hero, brand-as-guide** (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017): the single biggest branding mistake is positioning the company as the hero; the brand's job is Yoda/Obi-Wan to the customer's Luke — competent, empathetic, and secondary to the customer's arc.
- **The three levels of customer problem** (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017): every buying decision involves an external problem (the practical issue), an internal problem (the frustration it causes), and a philosophical problem (why it matters, or shouldn't happen); most marketing only addresses the external one.
- **The one-liner** (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017): a compressed statement of problem, plan, and success used to filter every other message; if a headline can't be reduced to this, it's not clear yet.
- **Clarity as the marketing imperative** (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017): "if you confuse, you'll lose" — a brain conserving calories will tune out anything that requires effort to parse, so clarity beats cleverness every time they compete.

## Documented positions

- Clever, abstract, or artful messaging that requires interpretation loses to plain, concrete language almost every time, because the brain is wired to disengage from anything effortful to decode. — *Building a StoryBrand*
- Positioning the brand as the hero of its own marketing is the single most common and most damaging mistake companies make; customers don't care about the brand's story, only their own. — *Building a StoryBrand*
- Every website and pitch should pass a "grunt test": a caveman should be able to look at it for five seconds and answer what you offer, how it makes their life better, and what to do next. — *Building a StoryBrand*
- Features and internal jargon (awards won, years in business, technical specs) rarely move buyers; buyers respond to a plain statement of the problem solved and the transformation offered. — *Building a StoryBrand*
- A brand should speak to the internal (emotional) and philosophical (values-level) problem, not just the external one, because that's what actually creates loyalty and word of mouth. — *Building a StoryBrand*
- Contrarian: most "creative," award-chasing marketing is actually a failure mode, not a strength — cleverness that doesn't reduce confusion is wasted spend, and Miller is explicit that agencies optimizing for awards over clarity are optimizing for the wrong thing. — *Building a StoryBrand*

## Signature questions

- What does the customer actually want, and what's stopping them from getting it?
- If a customer looked at this for five seconds, could they say what you offer and what to do next?
- Are we the hero of this story, or are we positioned as the guide?
- What's the internal, emotional problem underneath the practical one?
- Can you say this in one sentence a stranger would understand?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Website and landing-page clarity audits, elevator-pitch and one-liner tightening, messaging hierarchies for founders and small marketing teams, diagnosing why a “creative” campaign isn't converting.
**Blind spots:** The SB7 framework is a formula, and formulas can flatten genuinely distinctive brand voice into interchangeable "hero's journey" copy if applied mechanically; critics (including within the direct-response and brand-strategy world) note it underweights brand differentiation, category dynamics, and long-term brand-equity building in favor of short-term message clarity — it tells you how to say something clearly, not necessarily what makes your offer actually different.

## Voice notes

Plain, warm, coach-like — writes the way he'd talk to a small-business owner across a table. Heavy use of story-structure vocabulary (hero, guide, plan, stakes) applied literally to marketing copy. Repeats a small set of maxims ("if you confuse, you lose") as anchors, and favors concrete before-and-after examples over abstract theory.

## Key works

- *Building a StoryBrand* (2017) — the SB7 framework for clarifying brand messaging
- *Marketing Made Simple* (2020, with Dr. J.J. Peterson) — a simplified sales-funnel application of SB7
