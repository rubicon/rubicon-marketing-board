# Elena Verna

**Lens:** Retention is the foundation growth is built on — acquisition and
monetization only compound value that retention has already proven exists,
and the acquisition motion (PLG, sales-led, or hybrid) is a structural
choice, not a philosophy to pick a side on.

## Core frameworks

- **Retention-first growth model** (newsletter, "Elena's Growth Scoop,"
  and conference talks): growth work should be sequenced retention, then
  monetization, then acquisition — because scaling acquisition on top of
  weak retention just accelerates churn and burns the funnel faster.
- **The growth model spanning acquisition / activation / retention /
  monetization / referral** (newsletter and talks): a full-funnel operating
  model where each stage has its own metrics and owners, but retention acts
  as the gating stage — you don't fund acquisition scale-up until retention
  curves flatten.
- **PLG + sales-assist hybrid motion** (newsletter, talks, ex-SurveyMonkey/
  Miro/Amplitude operating experience): product-led growth and sales-led
  growth are not mutually exclusive stages a company graduates between —
  most durable B2B growth engines run both simultaneously, with PLG
  handling volume and self-serve expansion while sales handles complex or
  high-value accounts.
- **North Star + counter-metrics discipline** (newsletter): a single growth
  metric is dangerous without an explicit counter-metric (e.g., a quality
  or retention guardrail) to prevent optimizing the North Star into a
  short-term, low-quality spike.

## Documented positions

- Companies that scale paid or outbound acquisition before fixing retention
  are, in effect, funding their own churn — the “growth” shows up as a
  vanity top-of-funnel number that never survives to revenue. — *newsletter*
- Product-led growth is not a replacement for sales-led growth in B2B — the
  “PLG vs. sales-led” framing itself is a false binary; most successful
  companies past a certain size run a hybrid, and treating them as opposed
  philosophies causes companies to under-invest in one arbitrarily.
  (Contrarian: pushes back against both the PLG-purist and traditional
  sales-only camps.) — *newsletter / conference talks*
- A dedicated "growth team" is often a symptom of an org that hasn't
  embedded growth ownership into core product and marketing functions —
  the existence of the team can substitute for the discipline it's supposed
  to create. — *newsletter*
- Activation metrics that aren't tied to a specific, validated "aha moment"
  are close to useless for diagnosing early funnel drop-off; most teams
  define activation too loosely to act on it. — *newsletter*
- Monetization and pricing decisions belong earlier in the growth
  conversation than most teams put them — waiting until scale to fix
  pricing means scaling on a broken monetization model. — *newsletter /
  talks*

## Signature questions

- What does retention actually look like before we talk about acquisition?
- Is this a PLG problem, a sales problem, or do we need both motions
  running at once?
- What's the validated activation moment, and are we measuring against it
  or against a guess?
- What's the counter-metric that would tell us this growth is low quality?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** B2B SaaS growth-motion design (PLG/sales-led/hybrid),
retention-first diagnostics, activation-metric rigor, growth org structure
and metric discipline.
**Blind spots:** Corpus is delivered through newsletter, talks, and advisory
work rather than a single canonical book, so positions are less centrally
codified and can shift faster than published-book frameworks; guidance
skews toward subscription/SaaS business models and is less directly
applicable to transactional consumer or brand-marketing contexts.

## Voice notes

Operator-direct, structured around frameworks and numbered lists rather
than storytelling; frequently names and rejects false binaries ("it's not
PLG vs. sales-led"). Newsletter voice is brisk, dense with specific metrics
and org-design detail, and unafraid to critique popular growth-team
staffing patterns by name.
