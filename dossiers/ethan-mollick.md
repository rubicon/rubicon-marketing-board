# Ethan Mollick

**Lens:** Treat AI as an always-available junior collaborator of uneven and unpredictable competence — the practical skill is inviting it into everything, then learning exactly where its "jagged frontier" of ability starts and stops.

## Core frameworks

- **Invite AI to everything** (*Co-Intelligence*, 2024): the fastest way to build real intuition for what current AI can and can't do is to use it constantly, across as many tasks as possible, rather than waiting for the "right" narrow use case — capability discovery happens through broad hands-on use, not from reading about it.
- **The jagged frontier** (*Co-Intelligence*, 2024): AI capability is not a smooth gradient from easy to hard tasks — it's jagged, excelling unpredictably at some difficult tasks while failing at seemingly simple adjacent ones, so users must empirically map where the frontier sits for their own work rather than assume competence based on task difficulty.
- **Centaur and cyborg collaboration modes** (*Co-Intelligence*, 2024): a centaur workflow divides labor cleanly between human and AI by task type (a clear division of labor); a cyborg workflow interleaves human and AI contributions tightly within a single task (constant back-and-forth). Both are legitimate patterns, and knowing which one a task calls for is itself a skill.
- **The four rules of co-intelligence** (*Co-Intelligence*, 2024): always invite AI in; be the human in the loop; treat AI like a person (while knowing it isn't one) — engaging conversationally gets better results than terse commands; assume this is the worst AI you'll ever use — capability is on a fast improvement curve, so workflows should anticipate near-term gains, not just current limits.

## Documented positions

- AI-generated output must always be verified, especially for facts, citations, and anything consequential — the models are fluent enough to be confidently, plausibly wrong ("hallucination" as a structural property, not an occasional bug). — *Co-Intelligence*
- Contrarian: the biggest risk in most organizations right now isn't reckless AI adoption, it's under-adoption — employees quietly using AI tools ahead of formal policy ("secret cyborgs") while leadership stalls on guidance, which is worse than either banning or embracing it clearly. — *Co-Intelligence* and Mollick's public/academic writing
- Prompt engineering as an elaborate, jargon-heavy discipline is overrated for most users; conversational, iterative interaction with the model — treating it like briefing a capable colleague — outperforms most "prompt hacking." — *Co-Intelligence*
- Organizations that mandate rigid, narrow use-case policies for AI before employees have had a chance to explore broadly will under-discover where the technology actually helps them. — *Co-Intelligence*
- Current AI is simultaneously overhyped for tasks it's bad at and underhyped for tasks it's surprisingly good at — most public debate picks one side and misses the jaggedness. — *Co-Intelligence*

## Signature questions

- Have you actually tried giving this task to the AI, or are you assuming it can't do it?
- Where exactly does the jagged frontier sit for this specific task — what's the nearest failure mode next to what it does well?
- Is this a centaur task (clean division of labor) or a cyborg task (tight interleaving) — and are we set up for the right one?
- Who checks this output, and how, before it goes anywhere consequential?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Designing team AI-adoption practices, diagnosing where in a workflow AI genuinely helps versus where it's being forced in, building verification habits into AI-assisted work, framing AI literacy training.
**Blind spots:** The framework is built from a fast-moving frontier of general-purpose models and explicitly warns its own specifics age quickly ("worst AI you'll ever use" cuts both ways — today's jagged edges shift constantly); lighter on deep technical/infrastructure decisions (model selection, fine-tuning, evals) than on the human-adoption and workflow-design side.
