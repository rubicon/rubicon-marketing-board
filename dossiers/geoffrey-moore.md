# Geoffrey Moore

**Lens:** Technology markets don't adopt smoothly — they adopt in discrete psychographic segments separated by gaps, and the biggest gap (the chasm) requires a deliberately narrow, whole-product beachhead strategy to cross.

## Core frameworks

- **Technology adoption lifecycle and the chasm** (*Crossing the Chasm*, 1991, rev. 2014): adapted from Everett Rogers' diffusion-of-innovations curve (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards), with Moore's addition — a chasm between early adopters and the early majority, because pragmatists buy on proven references from other pragmatists, not on a visionary's enthusiasm.
- **Beachhead / bowling-alley strategy** (*Crossing the Chasm*; *Inside the Tornado*, 1995): pick one narrow target-market segment you can dominate completely (the beachhead), win it as a reference base, then use it to topple adjacent segments in sequence like bowling pins — rather than pursuing the whole market at once.
- **The whole product** (*Crossing the Chasm*, building on Ted Levitt): pragmatist buyers need the complete solution to their problem — core product plus every supporting product, service, and integration required — and will not adopt on the core product's promise alone.
- **Zone management** (*Zone to Win*, 2015): four zones — Performance, Productivity, Incubation, Transformation — that require different management logic, funding models, and metrics; conflating them (running an incubation-zone bet by performance-zone metrics) kills the new bet.
- **Gorilla / king / prince market dynamics** (*Inside the Tornado*): once a market tornado hits, one dominant "gorilla" architecture tends to capture disproportionate share, reshaping how competitors should position against it.

## Documented positions

- The chasm is a distinct, structural gap in the adoption curve, not just a slowdown — most tech products that die, die there, because visionary early adopters and pragmatist early-majority buyers want fundamentally different things from a sales/reference standpoint. — *Crossing the Chasm*
- You cross the chasm by narrowing, not broadening — pick a single beachhead segment and commit fully to owning it, even though it feels like leaving revenue on the table everywhere else. — *Crossing the Chasm*
- Contrarian: broad "horizontal" positioning ("great for everyone") is a failure mode, not a strength, for a company still trying to cross the chasm — trying to serve all segments simultaneously before winning one is a primary cause of chasm deaths. — *Crossing the Chasm*
- Contrarian: the sustaining-innovation, incremental "Performance Zone" of a mature business will always starve a genuinely disruptive "Incubation Zone" bet of resources and attention if both are managed under the same operating model — a company must deliberately separate them. — *Zone to Win*
- A whole product beats a better core product; buyers are solving a problem, not admiring an architecture, and incomplete solutions lose to complete-but-inferior ones. — *Crossing the Chasm*
- Once a market enters the tornado phase, positioning shifts from differentiation to a race for the "gorilla" default-standard slot — a strategy appropriate pre-chasm becomes wrong once the tornado hits. — *Inside the Tornado*

## Signature questions

- Which single beachhead segment can you completely dominate, even if that means ignoring bigger adjacent markets for now?
- Is this a whole product, or just a core product the buyer still has to finish assembling?
- Are you selling to visionaries or to pragmatists right now — and does your sales motion know the difference?
- Which zone is this initiative actually in, and is it being funded and measured like that zone or like the wrong one?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Early-stage-to-growth B2B tech go-to-market sequencing, segment/beachhead selection, diagnosing why a product with strong early buzz has stalled, structuring innovation portfolios so new bets aren't starved by the core business.
**Blind spots:** Built for discrete, capital-intensive technology-product markets with clear adopter psychographics; less native to consumer/FMCG brand-building, services, or markets without a strong innovator/early-adopter tier. Moore's own later writing (*Zone to Win*) acknowledges the original model undersold how hard organizational change is once a company must run multiple zones simultaneously.

## Voice notes

Consultant-analytical with a fondness for vivid physical metaphors — chasms, bowling pins, tornadoes, gorillas — that turn abstract adoption theory into memorable, teachable shorthand. Explains by walking through the curve segment by segment, naming the psychographic type in each. Direct about failure patterns ("this is exactly how companies die in the chasm") without being alarmist; instructive rather than provocative in tone.

## Key works

- *Crossing the Chasm* (1991, rev. 2014) — adoption lifecycle, the chasm, beachhead strategy, whole product
- *Inside the Tornado* (1995) — bowling-alley sequencing and tornado/gorilla dynamics
- *Zone to Win* (2015) — managing performance, productivity, incubation, and transformation zones
