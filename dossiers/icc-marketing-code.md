# ICC Advertising and Marketing Communications Code

The International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) Advertising and Marketing
Communications Code is the longest-standing global self-regulatory framework
for marketing ethics, first issued in 1937 and periodically consolidated and
updated since (the current consolidated edition dates to 2018, incorporating
earlier separate codes on sales promotion, sponsorship, direct marketing, and
digital marketing). It has no statutory force on its own, but it is the
reference framework most national advertising self-regulatory bodies (e.g.
the UK's ASA, and members of the European Advertising Standards Alliance)
build their own codes on, which gives it de facto authority far beyond a
single company's internal guidelines. Zora treats it as the floor, not the
ceiling — the baseline any campaign must clear before any brand-specific
ethics question is even worth asking.

## Principles

- **Legal, decent, honest, truthful** — the Code's foundational four-part
  test: every marketing communication must comply with the law, respect
  prevailing standards of decency, avoid dishonest claims or omissions, and
  be truthful in substance and impression, not merely truthful in isolated
  literal wording.
- **Identification of marketing communications** — a marketing
  communication must be clearly distinguishable as such; it must not
  disguise its true commercial purpose (this is the Code's core basis for
  disclosure requirements around native advertising, influencer content,
  and sponsored placements).
- **Substantiation** — claims capable of objective verification (performance
  claims, comparative claims, scientific or statistical claims) must be
  capable of substantiation before publication, not after challenge.
- **Fair comparison** — comparative marketing must not create confusion with
  competitors, discredit them unfairly, or take unfair advantage of a
  competitor's reputation.
- **Protection of privacy and personal data** — marketing communications and
  the data practices behind them (targeting, profiling, tracking) must
  respect individuals' privacy and comply with data-protection principles,
  not just data-protection law.
- **Special protection for children and other vulnerable groups** —
  heightened obligations apply where the audience includes children or
  otherwise vulnerable people: no exploitation of inexperience or credulity,
  extra care around health, safety, and social claims directed at or
  reasonably likely to reach them.
- **Responsibility** — the Code places responsibility for compliance on
  everyone involved in commissioning, creating, and placing a marketing
  communication — advertiser, agency, and publisher/platform — not solely on
  the brand.
- **Digital and AI-relevant guidance** (the 2018 consolidated edition
  extended coverage to digital and social media marketing; the 11th edition,
  September 2024, added specific guidance on AI in marketing, later expanded
  in the ICC's dedicated "Responsible AI in Marketing" guide): the
  identification, substantiation, and vulnerable-audience principles apply
  without weakening to AI-generated or AI-personalized content, synthetic
  media, and algorithmically targeted advertising — a synthetic spokesperson
  or AI-written claim is still subject to full "legal, decent, honest,
  truthful" scrutiny. Disclosure of AI use is not automatic: the mere use of
  generative AI does not by itself require disclosure, but disclosure is
  required whenever omitting it would create a misleading impression —
  most clearly where AI generates or materially alters the likeness of a
  real person, or where an AI-generated figure could be mistaken for a real
  endorser or authority (e.g. a doctor).

## How Zora applies this

Zora runs this as a pass/fail floor check before any brand-strategy
discussion, rating each finding by severity:

- **Blocking** — the campaign as described would fail "legal, decent,
  honest, truthful" outright (a false or unsubstantiated claim, undisclosed
  paid placement, exploitation of children/vulnerable audiences). This must
  be fixed before the board proceeds to strategy questions; no positioning
  or creative merit offsets a Code failure.
- **Material risk** — the claim or tactic is defensible only with
  substantiation, disclosure, or targeting changes not yet confirmed (e.g.
  a comparative claim without cited data, an AI-generated testimonial
  without disclosure). Flag and require resolution before launch.
- **Advisory** — the tactic clears the Code's floor but sits close to a
  documented line (e.g. borderline vulnerable-audience reach, aggressive but
  substantiated comparison) — noted so the board can decide deliberately
  rather than by default.
