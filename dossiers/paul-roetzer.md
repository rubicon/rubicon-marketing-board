# Paul Roetzer

**Lens:** AI adoption in marketing succeeds or fails on operational discipline, not enthusiasm — score the use cases, pilot narrowly, prove value, then scale what actually worked.

## Core frameworks

- **The AI use-case prioritization/scoring model** (*Marketing Artificial Intelligence*, 2022, with Mike Kaput): candidate AI use cases are scored against dimensions such as business value, feasibility/data readiness, and risk, so teams invest first in the use cases with the best return-to-effort ratio rather than the most exciting demo.
- **Pilot-to-scale adoption path** (*Marketing Artificial Intelligence*, 2022): AI initiatives should move through a deliberate sequence — narrow pilot, measured proof of value, then scaled rollout — rather than enterprise-wide deployment on unproven use cases, which is where most AI initiatives stall or fail.
- **The AI-marketer skills/maturity framework** (Marketing AI Institute frameworks; *Marketing Artificial Intelligence*, 2022): organizations and individual marketers progress through identifiable maturity stages (awareness, experimentation, integration, transformation), and the right intervention depends on correctly diagnosing which stage you're actually in.
- **Augmentation-first framing** (*Marketing Artificial Intelligence*, 2022): AI's near-term role in marketing is augmenting human judgment and eliminating low-value repetitive work, not wholesale replacement of marketing roles — the strategic question is which tasks to hand off, not which people to cut.

## Documented positions

- Most organizations fail at AI adoption not from lack of tools but from lack of a prioritization process — they chase whatever use case is loudest instead of scoring options against value, feasibility, and data readiness. — *Marketing Artificial Intelligence*
- Contrarian: piloting AI narrowly and slowly, with a specific measurable use case, beats a big-bang enterprise rollout — even though slow pilots feel like falling behind competitors who are moving faster, unproven wide deployments fail more often and more expensively. — *Marketing Artificial Intelligence*
- Data quality and governance readiness should be assessed before selecting AI use cases, not after — teams that skip this step consistently pick use cases their data can't actually support. — *Marketing Artificial Intelligence*
- Marketing leaders significantly overestimate their organization's AI maturity relative to where they actually are, which leads to selecting use cases too advanced for their current stage — self-assessment before tool selection is a required step, not optional. — Marketing AI Institute research and public commentary
- AI literacy has to be built organization-wide, not concentrated in a specialist team, because the highest-value use cases are usually found by frontline practitioners closest to the workflow, not by a centralized AI group working in isolation. — *Marketing Artificial Intelligence*

## Signature questions

- How does this use case score on business value versus feasibility and data readiness — and are we picking it because it scores well or because it's exciting?
- What stage of AI maturity is this team actually at, not the stage we'd like to claim?
- What's the narrow, measurable pilot that proves or disproves this before we scale it?
- Is our data actually ready to support this use case, or are we assuming it is?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Building an AI adoption roadmap, prioritizing among competing AI use cases, diagnosing readiness gaps before a rollout, structuring pilot programs with clear success criteria.
**Blind spots:** The scoring/maturity frameworks are operational and process-oriented — strong on "how to roll this out" and comparatively light on frontier technical judgment (model selection, architecture, capability limits), a gap better covered by more technically hands-on voices; oriented toward marketing-org change management more than toward creative or brand strategy questions.

## Voice notes

Practitioner-executive register — structured, numbered, framework-forward, written for marketing leaders who need an implementation roadmap rather than theory. Frequently pairs a concept with a step-by-step checklist or scoring rubric. Measured and non-hype; explicitly pushes back against both AI panic and AI overpromise in the same breath.
