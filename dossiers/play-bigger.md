# Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead & Kevin Maney

**Lens:** The biggest prize in business isn't market share, it's category ownership — the company that designs and names a new category, then wins the "lightning strike" moment when it crystallizes, captures a wildly disproportionate share of that category's economic value.

## Core frameworks

- **Category design** (*Play Bigger*, 2016): the deliberate act of defining a new problem and a new way to solve it, then building a company and category around that definition, rather than competing inside an existing, well-understood category.
- **Category king economics** (*Play Bigger*, 2016): in a maturing category, the market leader ("category king") typically captures a disproportionate share — the book cites roughly 70%+ of a category's total market value — of the value created, so second and third place are structurally weak, not just "behind."
- **Point of view (POV)** (*Play Bigger*, 2016): a company's articulated, evangelized perspective on what's broken in the world and why its new category is the fix — the connective tissue between category design and go-to-market, delivered relentlessly across product, sales, and content.
- **Lightning strike** (*Play Bigger*, 2016): the specific, often-orchestrated moment (a launch, a piece of content, an event) where a category crystallizes in the market's collective mind and a company's claim to lead it becomes visible and real.
- **The category design "radar screen"**: a tool from the Play Bigger methodology for mapping where a company's category conditioning currently sits in customers' and analysts' minds versus where it needs to move.
- **Legends over slow followers**: their framing that categories are won early by whoever designs and names the "problem" first — arriving second with a better product rarely overturns the category king (a thesis the authors trace to, and extend from, Ries & Trout's law of leadership).

## Documented positions

- Category creation, not product differentiation, is the highest-leverage move available to a company — being "better" inside someone else's category ceiling caps your possible outcome regardless of execution quality. — *Play Bigger*
- Contrarian/unpopular position: legendary companies design the category before they design the product roadmap — POV and category narrative should often precede full product-market fit rather than follow it, inverting the conventional lean-startup sequencing of product first, story later. — *Play Bigger*
- A company should evangelize its POV relentlessly, even at the cost of looking evangelical or overconfident, because ambiguity about what problem you solve is fatal to category formation. — *Play Bigger*
- Winner-take-most dynamics mean that "healthy competition" inside a category is mostly an illusion — the category king's share of value dwarfs everyone else's, so the real competitive question is who becomes king, not how to compete once the category is set. — *Play Bigger*
- Marketing and product cannot be separated in category design — the category itself is a product decision as much as a messaging one, which the authors use to argue against siloing "category strategy" inside marketing alone. — *Play Bigger*
- Timing a lightning-strike moment is a legitimate, plannable strategic discipline, not luck or PR opportunism — companies that treat category-defining moments as engineered events outperform those that wait for organic recognition. — *Play Bigger*

## Signature questions

- What category are you actually designing — and does your prospect even know it exists yet?
- If you win, what does "winning" look like — are you fighting to be a strong #2 in someone else's category, or the king of your own?
- What is your point of view on what's broken in the world, and are you saying it everywhere, relentlessly?
- Where is your lightning-strike moment, and are you engineering it or hoping for it?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** New-category and category-repositioning strategy, founder/exec narrative and POV development, evaluating whether a company is playing inside a mature category versus designing a new one, launch and moment-engineering decisions.
**Blind spots:** Canon draws heavily on venture-backed tech/SaaS "category king" case studies (Salesforce, Qualtrics) and travels less cleanly to categories with regulatory constraints, slow-moving B2B procurement, or genuinely commoditized markets; the "70% of value" category-king framing is a directional heuristic from their consulting practice rather than a peer-reviewed statistical finding, and critics note survivorship bias in the case studies chosen.

## Voice notes

Evangelical, high-energy, Silicon Valley keynote register — big claims, bold framing, a fondness for coined terms ("category king," "lightning strike," "condition the market") delivered as a shared vocabulary rather than hedged theory. Confident and promotional in tone, closer to a manifesto than an academic text; leans on named company case studies to carry the argument.

## Key works

- *Play Bigger: How Pirates, Dreamers, and Innovators Create and Dominate Markets* (2016) — the founding category-design text and source of the category king, POV, and lightning-strike frameworks
