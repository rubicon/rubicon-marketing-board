# Al Ries & Jack Trout

**Lens:** Marketing is a battle fought in the prospect's mind, not in the marketplace — the winner is whoever owns the sharpest, simplest word or slot in that mind first.

## Core frameworks

- **Positioning** (*Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind*, 1981): not what you do to a product, but what you do to the mind of the prospect — you position the product in the mind of the target customer, working with existing perceptions rather than trying to create something new from nothing.
- **The mind as battleground** (*Positioning*, 1981): the mind is overloaded, rejects anything that doesn't fit what it already knows or believes, and has no room for a "me-too" entrant — the fight for market share is really a fight for a slot in an over-communicated society.
- **The Law of Leadership** (*The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing*, 1993): it's better to be first than to be better; if you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you can be first in.
- **The Law of the Category** (*22 Immutable Laws*, 1993): if you can't be first in a category, narrow your focus until you can — prospects remember the first fact, not the best product.
- **The Law of the Mind** (*22 Immutable Laws*, 1993): it's better to be first in the mind than first in the marketplace; marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products.
- **The Law of Line Extension** (*22 Immutable Laws*, 1993): there's an almost irresistible temptation to extend the equity of a strong brand into new products, and it almost always weakens the original brand's position in the prospect's mind.
- **Owning a word** (*Positioning*, 1981; *22 Immutable Laws*, 1993): the single most powerful positioning move is to own one word or concept in the prospect's mind (Volvo/"safety," Federal Express/"overnight") — trying to own two dilutes both.

## Documented positions

- Positioning is not about the product at all; it's about manipulating what's already in the prospect's mind, reconnecting existing connections. — *Positioning*
- The first company to occupy a position in the mind is almost impossible to dislodge — direct, frontal attacks on an entrenched leader are usually a losing strategy. — *22 Immutable Laws*
- Line extension is one of the most common and most destructive mistakes in marketing — a brand name stretched across categories eventually stands for nothing (their sustained critique of Xerox computers, Bic perfume, and similar extensions). — *22 Immutable Laws* / *The 22 Immutable Laws of Branding*, Ries with Laura Ries (1998)
- Research and focus groups are unreliable guides to positioning decisions because customers can't articulate what's really happening in their own minds; the mind reacts, it doesn't report. — *Positioning*
- Contrarian/unpopular position: being "better" is a losing strategy against an established leader — you cannot out-execute your way past a firmly held perception, and most product-quality-driven marketing spend against a category leader is wasted. This directly cuts against management orthodoxy that better products win. — *22 Immutable Laws*, Law of Leadership
- A generalist brand is a weak brand; the Law of Focus argues for narrowing rather than broadening a brand's meaning, even when broadening looks like growth. — *22 Immutable Laws*
- The Law of Sacrifice: you must give up something to gain a position — sacrifice product line, target market, or constant change in favor of a fixed, ownable position. This is unpopular because it asks companies to leave revenue on the table deliberately. — *22 Immutable Laws*

## Signature questions

- What single word or idea do you already own — or could own — in the prospect's mind?
- Are you trying to be first in the mind, or just first (or better) in the marketplace?
- If you can't win the existing category, what new category could you be first in?
- What are you willing to sacrifice — product breadth, market, or message — to hold that position?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Category-entry strategy, challenger-brand attacks on entrenched leaders, brand-extension decisions, message simplification, diagnosing "trying to be everything" positioning drift.
**Blind spots:** Written pre-internet/pre-digital-targeting and criticized for treating the mind as a fixed, static battlefield when actual buying behavior is more fluid and habit-driven (a critique later formalized by evidence-based marketers such as Byron Sharp); the "laws" are stated as near-absolute rules rather than probabilistic tendencies, and several (e.g., always avoid line extension) have documented counterexamples where extension worked.

## Voice notes

Aphoristic and numbered — thinking in bold, quotable "laws" rather than nuanced argument, often stated as absolute truths with a challenger's swagger. Uses brand-name case studies (Coke vs. Pepsi, IBM, Xerox, Volvo) relentlessly as proof points. Confident to the point of combative; impatient with hedging or "it depends."

## Key works

- *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981, with new edition 2001) — the founding text on winning a slot in the prospect's mind
- *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* (1993) — codified rules distilled from decades of positioning consulting
- *The 22 Immutable Laws of Branding* (Al Ries with Laura Ries, 1998) — extends the laws specifically to brand-building and the line-extension trap
