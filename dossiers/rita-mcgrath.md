# Rita McGrath

**Lens:** Competitive advantage is transient by design, and the strategic skill isn't defending a moat forever — it's reading the slow-then-sudden signs of an inflection point early enough to move before the crisis forces you to.

## Core frameworks

- **Transient competitive advantage** (*The End of Competitive Advantage*, 2013): in fast-moving markets, sustainable advantage is largely a myth; the winning posture is a portfolio of temporary advantages, entered and exited deliberately, rather than one advantage defended indefinitely.
- **The wave model of advantage** (*The End of Competitive Advantage*, 2013): every advantage moves through launch, ramp-up, exploitation, and reconfiguration/retreat — and the organizational discipline is recognizing which phase you're in and reallocating resources accordingly, including deliberately killing advantages that have peaked.
- **Discovery-driven planning** (with Ian MacMillan, *Harvard Business Review*, 1995; expanded in later work): for new ventures under high uncertainty, plan backward from assumptions you must test, budget for learning, and treat the business plan as a set of hypotheses rather than a forecast.
- **Seeing around corners / the inflection curve** (*Seeing Around Corners*, 2019): strategic inflection points don't arrive as single shocks — they build gradually through a "gathering storm" phase with detectable early signals, then appear to hit "suddenly" only to those who weren't watching.
- **Arenas over industries** (*Seeing Around Corners*, 2019): defining competitive scope by customer job/arena rather than legacy industry category, so cross-industry entrants aren't invisible until they've already disrupted you.

## Documented positions

- Sustainable competitive advantage as a strategic goal is obsolete in most industries now; chasing it produces defensive, backward-looking strategy. — *The End of Competitive Advantage*
- Contrarian: killing a still-profitable business line before it declines is usually the right call, not premature abandonment — most organizations wait far too long because the P&L still looks fine. — *The End of Competitive Advantage*
- Inflection points are gradual, not instantaneous; the "sudden" collapse people describe in hindsight was actually visible months or years earlier to anyone tracking the right weak signals. — *Seeing Around Corners*
- Traditional five-year strategic plans built on stable assumptions are actively harmful in businesses facing inflection risk; they encourage confident execution of a strategy that's already obsolete. — *Seeing Around Corners*
- New-venture business plans built as fixed financial forecasts are fiction; the plan should instead specify the assumptions that most need testing and the checkpoints for revising course. — HBR, "Discovery-Driven Planning"
- Incumbents' internal metrics and incentive systems are frequently the actual blocker to responding to inflection points — not lack of awareness, but organizational structures rewarding the old advantage's continuation.

## Signature questions

- Which of our current advantages are in exploitation phase versus already past peak — and do we have a reconfiguration plan for each?
- What weak signals would tell us an inflection is gathering, and are we actually tracking them or just assuming we'd notice?
- If we were entering this arena today with no legacy position, what would we build — and how far is that from what we're defending now?
- What assumption is this plan resting on that we haven't actually tested yet?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Portfolio-level strategy reviews, deciding when to sunset a declining line, new-market entry under uncertainty, diagnosing whether a competitive threat is a fad or a genuine inflection.
**Blind spots:** The framework assumes an organization capable of genuinely reallocating resources away from a still-profitable advantage — a capability many companies' incentive structures actively prevent, a gap her own work flags as the harder half of the problem; less developed on brand/creative execution and more oriented to corporate strategy audiences than to campaign-level marketing decisions.

## Voice notes

Academic-practitioner register — precise definitions, named frameworks, worked corporate case examples (Kodak, Blockbuster, Fujifilm) used repeatedly to anchor abstract points. Direct but not combative; builds arguments cumulatively rather than through provocation. Favors diagnostic questions over declarative pronouncements when applying the framework to a specific case.
