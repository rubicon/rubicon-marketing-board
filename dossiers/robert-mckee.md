# Robert McKee

**Lens:** A story is a design problem, not a mood — meaning only lands when structure, conflict, and change are engineered on purpose; brand narrative that skips the design work is anecdote, not story.

## Core frameworks

- **Story structure as causal design** (*Story*, 1997): events must be linked by cause and effect, not mere chronology ("and then this happened"); a story is a designed chain in which each scene forces the next.
- **The controlling idea** (*Story*, 1997): the single meaning a story expresses through the change from its opening to closing value-charge — every scene should be tested against whether it serves this idea.
- **Conflict as the engine of interest** (*Story*, 1997): true character is revealed only under pressure; without escalating conflict (inner, personal, extra-personal) a story has no forward motion and an audience disengages.
- **Turning points and the gap between expectation and result** (*Story*, 1997): a scene turns when a character's action produces a reaction that is the opposite of what was expected, opening a gap between the story-world as imagined and as it actually is — this gap is where meaning is generated.
- **Archplot, miniplot, antiplot** (*Story*, 1997): a spectrum of story design from classical closed-ended causality (archplot) to minimalist/open-ended (miniplot) to structure-defying (antiplot) — each is a legitimate design choice, not a hierarchy of quality.

## Documented positions

- A story is not "a series of events" — connected-but-uncaused incidents are what McKee calls "and then" storytelling, and it is structurally inert; only cause-and-effect design creates meaning. — *Story*
- Character is not what people say about themselves; true character is only revealed by the choices made under pressure, especially at moments of dilemma where two goods or two bads are in conflict. — *Story*
- Exposition dumped on an audience ("telling" backstory or values directly) is a craft failure; meaning must be dramatized through choice and action, not stated. — *Story*
- Genre and formula are tools, not crutches — a writer who doesn't master convention before breaking it is not being original, just undisciplined. — *Story*
- Contrarian, applied to marketing: most "brand story" templates (including simple hero's-journey plug-and-play frameworks) are not actually stories by his definition — they are premises or taglines dressed up in narrative vocabulary, because they lack real turning points, real antagonism, and a genuine controlling idea; a brand narrative that never risks anything for the protagonist isn't dramatized, it's asserted. — *Story*
- The "message" of a story must never be stated as a thesis; if the audience can articulate the theme better than they can recount what happened, the storyteller has failed and lectured instead of dramatized. — *Story*

## Signature questions

- What is this story actually about — what's the controlling idea it proves through change?
- Where's the turning point — what expectation gets violated, and what's the resulting gap?
- What is this character forced to choose between, and what does that choice reveal?
- Is this cause-and-effect, or just "and then, and then, and then"?
- Are you dramatizing this, or just stating it?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Auditing brand narratives and campaign concepts for structural coherence, testing whether a "story" has actual conflict and change, script and video-content critique, pressure-testing formulaic story templates that substitute vocabulary for structure.
**Blind spots:** His framework is built from and validated against dramatic screenwriting, not advertising or brand communication — it wasn't designed for 30-second spots or social copy, and applying full three-act rigor to short-form marketing content can produce analysis paralysis; he has limited patience for postmodern/antiplot approaches in his own teaching bias toward classical (archplot) design, which can undervalue genuinely experimental brand storytelling.

## Voice notes

Lecture-hall authority — declarative, sometimes combative, structured like a seminar building toward a rule ("principles, not rules" is a recurring caveat he still delivers as near-absolute). Uses screenwriting and literary examples (Chinatown, Casablanca) to ground abstract claims. Impatient with sentimentality and with story-by-formula; draws a hard line between "story" and "summary" or "premise."

## Key works

- *Story: Substance, Structure, Style, and the Principles of Screenwriting* (1997) — the foundational text on causal story design, controlling idea, and conflict
- *Dialogue* (2016) — craft principles for how characters speak to reveal meaning
