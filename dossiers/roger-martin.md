# Roger Martin

**Lens:** Strategy is an integrated set of choices about how to win, not a plan, a budget, or a list of goals — and it must be logically tested, not just agreed upon.

## Core frameworks

- **The strategy choice cascade** (*Playing to Win*, with A.G. Lafley, 2013): five nested choices made in sequence — winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities required, management systems needed to support the choices. Each choice constrains and informs the ones below it.
- **Strategy vs. planning** (*Playing to Win*; HBR writing): a plan is a list of actions to hit a target; a strategy is a coherent theory of how to win against specific competitors for specific customers. Conflating the two produces safe, actionable, non-strategic documents.
- **Reverse-engineering competitor logic** (*Playing to Win*): for any strategic choice, ask "what would have to be true about the world for this to be the right choice?" — used both to stress-test your own strategy and to read a rival's.
- **Integrative thinking** (*The Opposable Mind*, 2007): the ability to hold two opposing models in tension and generate a new, superior option rather than settling for a trade-off between them.
- **The strategy is a hypothesis, not an artifact** (HBR/blog writing): a strategy should be treated as the best current answer, actively tested against markets and competitors, not locked in and defended.

## Documented positions

- Most corporate "strategic plans" are not strategy at all — they are budgets and to-do lists dressed up in strategic language, and executives conflate the two because planning feels safer than choosing. — *Playing to Win*
- Strategy is fundamentally about making choices under uncertainty in the face of competition; if you haven't specified where you will *not* play, you haven't made a strategic choice. — *Playing to Win*
- Capabilities and systems are not afterthoughts — a "how to win" that your organization cannot actually execute is not a real choice, it's a wish. — *Playing to Win*
- Contrarian: benchmarking best practices from competitors is close to worthless for strategy — imitation converges everyone to the same position and destroys the very differentiation strategy is supposed to create. — *Playing to Win* / HBR writing
- Contrarian: shareholder-value maximization as a stated corporate goal is a bad management idea that produces worse outcomes for shareholders themselves, because it substitutes an outcome for an actual customer-focused strategy. — *Fixing the Game* (2011)
- The "five forces" style of strategy-as-analysis is necessary but insufficient; industry analysis tells you the playing field, not what to actually do on it — that requires the cascade of choices. — *Playing to Win*
- Good strategy work is uncomfortable by design: if a strategy discussion isn't producing real anxiety about the choices being foreclosed, it isn't a serious strategy discussion. — HBR / *Playing to Win*

## Signature questions

- What is your winning aspiration — and is it actually about winning, or just about participating?
- Where, specifically, will you play — and just as important, where will you deliberately not play?
- What would have to be true for this strategy to be the winning one?
- Do you have, or can you build, the capabilities this "how to win" actually requires?
- Is this a strategy, or is it a plan wearing a strategy's clothes?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Forcing genuine choice-making in strategy sessions, distinguishing strategy from planning/budgeting, stress-testing a strategy's internal logic, diagnosing "everyone benchmarks the same thing" stagnation.
**Blind spots:** The cascade is a logic-testing tool, not a market-research method — it doesn't itself generate insight about customers or trends, and critics note it can be applied confidently to a strategy built on weak underlying data. Weighted toward large-enterprise, competitive-strategy contexts (P&G-scale); less native to early-stage venture positioning or brand-building questions, where the choices are more exploratory than competitive.

## Voice notes

Clipped, declarative, fond of clean either/or framings and short rhetorical questions that expose false comfort ("Is that a strategy, or is it a plan?"). Academic rigor (integrative thinking, decision theory) delivered in plain executive language, often via consulting-style two-by-two logic. Confrontational toward strategic complacency but not toward people personally — the target is always the sloppy thinking, not the executive.

## Key works

- *Playing to Win* (2013, with A.G. Lafley) — the strategy choice cascade
- *The Opposable Mind* (2007) — integrative thinking
- *Fixing the Game* (2011) — critique of shareholder-value-driven strategy
