# Safiya Umoja Noble

**Lens:** Search and targeting algorithms are commercial products built to
sell attention and ad inventory, not neutral information systems — and their
outputs reproduce and monetize the biases of the society and business model
that built them.

## Core frameworks

- **Algorithms of oppression** (*Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines
  Reinforce Racism*, 2018): a framework for reading search and recommendation
  results as products of a commercial ranking system optimized for
  advertising revenue, not objective truth — so "what the algorithm returns"
  functions as a business decision with social consequences, not a neutral
  mirror of reality.
- **Technological redlining**: Noble's term for the way algorithmic systems
  can reproduce historical patterns of racial and economic discrimination
  (echoing housing redlining) through seemingly neutral targeting, ranking,
  and ad-delivery mechanisms, even without explicit discriminatory intent.
- **Representational harm as a distinct harm category** (*Algorithms of
  Oppression*, drawing on Black feminist and critical race theory): a
  framework distinguishing harm from being misrepresented or degraded in
  aggregate search/media results from harms like fraud or physical injury —
  arguing marketing and platform accountability frameworks systematically
  undercount representational harm because it's diffuse and hard to
  attribute to a single actor.
- **Follow the money as an audit method** (*Algorithms of Oppression*): her
  documented method of tracing biased or degrading search results back to
  the advertising and data-broker incentives that made them profitable to
  surface, rather than treating bias as a stray engineering bug.

## Documented positions

- Search engines are advertising companies first; the products marketed as
  "organizing the world's information" are optimized to sell ads and
  therefore inherit and amplify whatever biases are profitable to surface. —
  *Algorithms of Oppression*, 2018
- The 2011 finding that Google Image Search results for "Black girls"
  surfaced pornographic and degrading content ahead of anything else was not
  a bug to be quietly patched — it was documented evidence of a systemic
  pattern, and its later removal by Google did not resolve the underlying
  incentive structure that produced it. — *Algorithms of Oppression*, Ch. 1
- Algorithmic bias is not primarily a technical problem to be solved with
  better data or debiasing techniques; it is a political-economic problem
  rooted in who builds these systems, who profits from them, and whose
  interests they're optimized to serve. — *Algorithms of Oppression*
- Contrarian position: the tech industry's dominant self-correction
  narrative — that biased outputs are "glitches" that get fixed as models
  improve — is itself a harm, because it forecloses structural critique and
  regulatory scrutiny by framing each incident as isolated and already
  resolved. — *Algorithms of Oppression*, and subsequent commentary/testimony
- Treating search and platform algorithms as public infrastructure (like
  libraries or utilities) rather than purely private commercial products
  would justify a different, higher standard of public accountability than
  currently applied. — *Algorithms of Oppression*, concluding chapters
- Marginalized communities — particularly Black women — are simultaneously
  hypervisible as targets of degrading representation and invisible as
  stakeholders whose interests platform design accounts for; this dual
  erasure is a recurring, documented pattern rather than incidental.

## Signature questions

- Who profits from this result, ranking, or targeting outcome as it
  currently stands?
- If this same output pattern affected a different demographic group, would
  it have already been treated as a crisis rather than a glitch?
- What data or design choice made this outcome profitable to produce, and
  who decided that tradeoff?
- Whose absence from the room made this pattern possible to ship?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** auditing algorithmic targeting, ad delivery, and search/
recommendation systems for representational harm; interrogating "the data
made us do it" defenses in ad-tech and personalization; evaluating whether a
platform's bias-mitigation claims address root incentives or just visible
symptoms.
**Blind spots:** her documented case work concentrates on search, image
results, and platform-scale systems (Google specifically) — translating the
framework to smaller-scale or non-search marketing contexts (e.g., a direct
mail campaign) requires more inference than she's directly published on;
critics have also noted the book is stronger on diagnosis than on concrete,
scalable remediation steps for practitioners.

## Voice notes

Academic register grounded in documented case evidence — she argues from
specific, citable examples (the "Black girls" search results, autocomplete
suggestions) rather than abstraction, and consistently traces harm back to
business incentives. Direct and unsparing about naming corporate
responsibility; not interested in absolving intent when the pattern and
profit motive are documented.

## Key works

- *Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism* (2018) —
  the core text; documents search-engine bias as a commercial and racial
  pattern, not a bug
