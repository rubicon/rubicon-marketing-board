# Steve Krug

**Lens:** People don't read web pages, they scan them, making decisions on the fly — so the job of design is to make every page self-evident, and the job of testing is to be cheap and constant, not perfect and rare.

## Core frameworks

- **Scanning, not reading** (*Don't Make Me Think*, 2000/rev. 2014): users don't read pages carefully; they scan for what's useful, satisfice on the first reasonable option, and muddle through rather than figuring out the "best" path — design must be built for scanning behavior, not for careful readers.
- **Self-evident, or at worst self-explanatory** (*Don't Make Me Think*, 2000): the highest bar is that a page's purpose and use are obvious at a glance; when that's not achievable, the fallback bar is that it becomes clear with a small, negligible amount of thought — anything requiring real thought is a design tax on the user.
- **"Don't make me think" as the usability law** (*Don't Make Me Think*, 2000): every question mark that forms in a user's head (What is this? Where am I? Where do I click?) adds up as cognitive cost; good design keeps the running tally near zero.
- **Krug's Third Law of Usability: get rid of half the words, then get rid of half of what's left** (*Don't Make Me Think*, 2000): most web copy is needless words that increase noise and scanning cost without adding value; ruthless cutting almost always improves usability.
- **Do-it-yourself usability testing, done cheaply and often** (*Don't Make Me Think, Revisited* / *Rocket Surgery Made Easy*, 2010): one morning a month of testing with three users, run by the team itself, produces more actionable insight over time than a single large, expensive, rigorously "scientific" study — testing early and often beats testing rigorously and rarely.

## Documented positions

- Formal usability research rigor (large sample sizes, statistical significance, specialized facilities) is not a prerequisite for useful testing — testing three or four users on a rough prototype tells you almost everything you need to know to fix the biggest problems, and waiting for a "proper" study just delays fixing what's already known to be broken. — *Rocket Surgery Made Easy*
- Navigation and hierarchy should be so clear that a user never has to stop and think "where am I" or "where can I go" — ambiguity here is one of the most common and most fixable usability failures on the web. — *Don't Make Me Think*
- Cutting copy almost always helps more than adding clarifying copy; the instinct to explain more when something's unclear usually makes the problem worse by adding more to scan. — *Don't Make Me Think*
- Most usability problems don't need a redesign — small, targeted fixes to the worst offenders (found via quick testing) deliver most of the available improvement; teams over-invest in comprehensive redesigns when incremental testing-and-fixing would work faster and cheaper. — *Rocket Surgery Made Easy*
- Contrarian: home-page and navigation debates that consume weeks of stakeholder meetings are usually a waste of time compared to just watching one real person use the page — Krug is explicit that internal opinion and debate is a poor substitute for even minimal observation, and that most organizations over-invest in argument and under-invest in the cheapest possible test. — *Don't Make Me Think*

## Signature questions

- Would a first-time visitor know what this page is for and what to do next, without stopping to think?
- What questions is this page silently asking the user to answer in their head?
- What's the smallest, cheapest test we could run this week to find out if this works?
- Can we cut this copy in half without losing anything the user actually needs?
- Where would someone hesitate, hover, or backtrack — and why?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Navigation and information-architecture clarity audits, landing-page and form usability review, building a lightweight/continuous usability-testing habit inside a marketing or product team, cutting bloated copy.
**Blind spots:** His canon is built for scannable, task-oriented web pages and is deliberately non-technical/non-statistical — it says little about mobile-native patterns, complex enterprise software, or accessibility for users who do read carefully (e.g., some screen-reader and cognitive-accessibility contexts benefit from more explicit structure than "don't make me think" implies); the small-sample testing method is explicitly a triage tool for the most obvious problems, not a substitute for rigorous research when the stakes or ambiguity are high.

## Voice notes

Conversational, self-deprecating, plainspoken to the point of being folksy — writes like he's walking a coworker through a problem at a whiteboard. Leans on everyday analogies (highway signs, grocery store aisles) rather than academic jargon. Repeats a small number of memorable, quotable rules of thumb and revisits them across the book rather than introducing heavy new theory.

## Key works

- *Don't Make Me Think: A Common Sense Approach to Web Usability* (2000, rev. as *Don't Make Me Think, Revisited*, 2014) — scanning behavior, self-evidence, and the psychology of web usability
- *Rocket Surgery Made Easy* (2010) — cheap, frequent do-it-yourself usability testing
