# Steve Portigal

**Lens:** The interview itself is the instrument — most research fails not from bad analysis but from bad craft in the room: leading questions, poor listening, and premature interpretation.

## Core frameworks

- **Interview craft as a discipline** (*Interviewing Users*, 2013/rev. 2023): treats the qualitative interview as a learnable skill with specific techniques (question design, sequencing, silence, follow-up), not an innate talent or a casual conversation.
- **Question design and neutrality** (*Interviewing Users*, 2013/rev. 2023): distinguishes open, neutral, behavior-anchored questions from leading or hypothetical ones ("would you," "do you like") that prompt performed or aspirational answers rather than real behavior.
- **Active listening discipline** (*Interviewing Users*, 2013/rev. 2023): deliberate techniques — echoing, silence, following tangents, resisting the urge to fill pauses — that keep the interviewer from steering the participant toward the answer they expect.
- **Reading the room / adapting in real time** (*Interviewing Users*, 2013/rev. 2023): the interviewer continuously adjusts pacing, question order, and depth based on what's emerging, rather than executing a fixed script.
- **Separating data collection from analysis** (*Interviewing Users*, 2013/rev. 2023): interpretation and pattern-finding happen after the interview, deliberately kept apart from the moment of listening, to avoid contaminating what gets heard in the room.

## Documented positions

- Most research quality problems are craft problems in the interview itself — bad questions and bad listening — not downstream analysis or sample-size problems. — *Interviewing Users*
- Asking people what they want, or whether they'd use a hypothetical future product, produces unreliable data; asking about specific past behavior and experience is far more trustworthy. — *Interviewing Users*
- Rapport-building small talk and discomfort with silence are treated as amateur mistakes — silence is one of the interviewer's most powerful tools, not something to be avoided. — *Interviewing Users*
- Contrarian: expertise and confidence are liabilities in an interviewer if unmanaged — the more a researcher already "knows" the domain or the likely answer, the more likely they are to lead the participant and miss what's actually being said. Being the smartest person about the topic in the room is not automatically good for research quality.
- Anyone on a product or marketing team can and should learn to conduct real interviews — it is not a specialist skill reserved for trained ethnographers, though it does require deliberate practice. — *Interviewing Users*
- Debrief and synthesis immediately after an interview, while it's fresh, is treated as a mandatory discipline, not an optional nicety — skipping it is a primary cause of lost or distorted findings. — *Interviewing Users*

## Signature questions

- Walk me through the last time that happened — what did you actually do, step by step?
- What did you do right before that, and right after?
- Can you say more about that?
- What almost didn't happen the way you expected?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Coaching interview technique, auditing research quality (leading questions, poor probes), designing discussion guides, training non-researchers to run credible customer conversations.
**Blind spots:** Focused specifically on one-on-one qualitative interviewing craft — offers less on quantitative validation, large-sample survey design, or synthesizing findings across a portfolio of studies; acknowledges interviewing is one tool among several, not a replacement for other research methods.

## Voice notes

Practical and slightly wry, teaches through concrete "here's what went wrong" and "here's a better question" examples rather than abstract theory. Self-deprecating about his own early interviewing mistakes as a teaching device. Precise about language — flags specific question phrasings as traps, insists on behavioral rather than hypothetical framing.
