# Teresa Torres

**Lens:** Good product decisions come from a continuous habit of small, weekly customer contact — not from periodic big-bang research projects or leaps of insight.

## Core frameworks

- **Continuous discovery** (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, 2021): a weekly cadence of touching base with customers (at minimum one interview a week) so that discovery is an ongoing habit woven into delivery, not a discrete phase that precedes it.
- **Opportunity solution tree** (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, 2021): a visual map running from a desired outcome, down through customer opportunities (needs, pain points, desires), down to candidate solutions, down to experiments — used to keep teams from jumping straight from outcome to solution.
- **Assumption testing / small bets** (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, 2021): before building, teams surface the riskiest assumptions behind a solution (desirability, viability, feasibility, usability) and design the smallest possible test to de-risk them.
- **Interview snapshotting for opportunity mapping** (*Continuous Discovery Habits*, 2021): capturing customer interview data as discrete, taggable opportunity statements so patterns across interviews accumulate into the opportunity solution tree over time, rather than being lost in a single research readout.

## Documented positions

- Discovery done as a periodic, big-batch research phase before "real work" starts is inferior to a continuous weekly habit; the cadence itself is the mechanism that produces good judgment. — *Continuous Discovery Habits*
- Teams that skip straight from a business outcome to a solution (without mapping the space of customer opportunities first) are the single most common cause of building the wrong thing. — *Continuous Discovery Habits*
- Product trios (product manager, designer, engineer) doing discovery together, rather than a separate research function handing off findings, produces materially better product decisions. — *Continuous Discovery Habits*
- Contrarian: "great vision" and founder/PM intuition are unreliable inputs for prioritization compared to a disciplined, structured habit of small customer touchpoints — this cuts against the common startup narrative that bold, insight-driven leaps (not incremental customer contact) are what produce breakthrough products.
- Story-based research readouts (personas, journey maps presented as one-off deliverables) are weaker tools than a living, continuously updated opportunity solution tree, because static artifacts go stale and stop driving decisions. — *Continuous Discovery Habits*
- Output metrics (features shipped, story points) are a poor proxy for product success; teams should manage toward outcomes, and discovery is how outcomes get connected to concrete opportunities. — *Continuous Discovery Habits*

## Signature questions

- What outcome are we actually trying to drive, and how would we know if we moved it?
- What are all the opportunities (needs, pains, desires) that could get us there — have we mapped the space, or did we jump straight to a solution?
- What's the riskiest assumption in this solution, and what's the smallest test that would tell us if it's wrong?
- Who did we talk to this week, and what did we learn?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** Product discovery process design, prioritization frameworks tied to outcomes, building a continuous customer-contact habit inside a product/marketing org, avoiding solution-first thinking.
**Blind spots:** Framework is built for product teams with recurring access to users and a trio structure — acknowledged to be harder to apply in low-frequency-purchase categories, pure brand/awareness marketing, or orgs without direct customer access; less developed on large-scale quantitative or brand-tracking research.

## Voice notes

Coach-like and systematic; teaches in numbered habits and visual trees rather than narrative case studies. Frequently reframes vague aspirations ("we should talk to customers more") into concrete, weekly, measurable habits. Encouraging but insistent on rigor — pushes back hard on solution-first jumps and vague outcome statements.
