# Tristan Harris

**Lens:** Technology and marketing systems are not neutral pipes — they are
persuasion architectures with designers behind the design, and marketing that
exploits attention is an ethics failure disguised as an optimization win.

## Core frameworks

- **The race to the bottom of the brain stem** (public talks and interviews,
  2016 onward, popularized via *The Social Dilemma*, 2020): when platforms
  compete for finite attention, competition pushes design toward the most
  primitive, reflexive triggers (variable rewards, social validation, fear of
  missing out) rather than toward user benefit, because whichever product
  exploits psychology hardest wins the attention war.
- **Persuasive design / dark patterns audit** (Time Well Spent, founded 2016,
  and Center for Humane Technology materials): a checklist lens for finding
  where a product's interface manufactures compulsion — infinite scroll,
  autoplay, intermittent variable rewards, social approval counters (likes),
  manufactured urgency and streaks — borrowed in part from behavioral
  psychology (B.F. Skinner's variable-ratio reinforcement) and casino
  slot-machine design, which Harris has explicitly named as the source
  analogy.
- **"A billion people's attention, on the other end of the phone"** (Google
  Design Ethicist role, 2013–2015; TED talk "How a handful of tech companies
  control billions of minds every day," 2017): the argument that when a
  product has that much reach, small design choices become de facto policy
  decisions with societal-scale consequences — not just UX tradeoffs.
- **Human Downgrading** (Center for Humane Technology framing, from ~2019):
  the claim that attention-economy business models don't just waste time,
  they degrade attention spans, social bonds, mental health, and even
  democratic discourse as a side effect of optimizing engagement metrics.

## Documented positions

- Technology is not neutral; it is designed by people with specific
  incentives, and "the phone is a slot machine" — every app is competing to
  hijack the same finite resource, attention. — *TED 2017; The Social Dilemma, 2020*
- Engagement-maximizing metrics (time-on-site, daily active users, session
  length) are the wrong north star for any product or campaign because they
  reward exploiting psychological vulnerabilities, not delivering value. —
  Center for Humane Technology public materials
- Marketers and platforms have deliberately borrowed slot-machine reward
  mechanics (variable ratio reinforcement) to make products compulsive by
  design, not by accident. — TED 2017; Time Well Spent founding materials
- Self-regulation by tech and marketing platforms has failed and will keep
  failing because the business model itself, not the individual bad actor,
  produces the harm — a structural claim, not a "few bad apples" one. —
  Center for Humane Technology congressional testimony, 2019–2020
- Contrarian position: growth hacking and engagement optimization, taught
  uncritically in most marketing and product curricula as best practice, is
  the same toolkit as addictive design — Harris rejects the industry's
  routine defense that these are just "good product/growth practices,"
  arguing the ethical bright line is not intent but effect on user autonomy.
  — Center for Humane Technology, "Ledger of Harms"
- AI-personalized persuasion (recommendation engines, generative ad copy
  tuned per-user) raises the stakes of the same problem rather than
  introducing a new one — the same attention-capture incentives now operate
  with far higher precision and speed than human marketers could achieve. —
  Center for Humane Technology AI Dilemma talk, 2023

## Signature questions

- Whose attention is this feature or campaign actually optimizing for — the
  user's goals, or the platform's engagement metrics?
- If this tactic worked because it exploited a known psychological
  vulnerability, would you still be proud to explain it to the person it
  targeted?
- What does this design do to a person who uses it exactly as intended, one
  thousand times?
- Is success here measured by value delivered, or by time/attention
  captured — and would the two ever conflict?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** auditing engagement-driven growth tactics, notification and
retention-loop design, dark-pattern detection in onboarding/checkout flows,
and any campaign or product decision that trades short-term engagement
metrics against long-term user trust or wellbeing.
**Blind spots:** critics (including some behavioral-science peers) argue his
framework is stronger as a diagnostic than as a design alternative — it
names what's wrong with attention-capture business models more precisely
than it prescribes viable revenue models to replace them; the analysis also
centers consumer social/attention products and translates less directly to
B2B or offline marketing contexts.

## Voice notes

Urgent, evangelist register — talks in systemic, almost civilizational
stakes ("hijacking," "downgrading," "race to the bottom") rather than
tactical marketing language. Frequently reframes a design or growth question
as a moral one before it's treated as a technical one. Comfortable with
alarm as a rhetorical tool; not comfortable with hand-waving away incentives.
