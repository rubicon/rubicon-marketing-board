# W. Edwards Deming

**Lens:** Nearly every recurring performance problem is a system problem,
not a people problem — management's job is to improve the system, not to
inspect, rank, or exhort the people working inside it.

## Core frameworks

- **Common cause vs. special cause variation** (*Out of the Crisis*, 1986):
  common-cause variation is the routine noise built into a stable system
  (normal fluctuation in response rates, conversion, cycle time); special-
  cause variation is a specific, assignable disruption. Treating common
  cause as if it were special cause — reacting to every up-and-down blip as
  news — makes performance worse, not better (tampering).
- **The 94/6 rule** (*Out of the Crisis*, 1986): Deming's estimate that
  roughly 94% of problems and possibilities for improvement belong to the
  system (management's responsibility) and only about 6% to the individual;
  he considered this the single most important management idea in the book.
- **PDSA cycle — Plan, Do, Study, Act** (*Out of the Crisis*, 1986; refined
  from Walter Shewhart's "Shewhart cycle"): a continuous, iterative learning
  loop for improvement — plan a change, run it at small scale, study what
  actually happened against prediction, then act on what was learned. Deming
  insisted on "Study," not the more common "Check," because the point is
  learning, not verification of compliance.
- **System of Profound Knowledge** (*The New Economics*, 1993): four
  interdependent lenses — appreciation for a system, knowledge of variation,
  theory of knowledge, and psychology — that together form what a manager
  needs to understand before intervening in an organization.
- **The 14 Points for Management** (*Out of the Crisis*, 1986): a set of
  management obligations including constancy of purpose, driving out fear,
  breaking down barriers between departments, and eliminating slogans and
  numerical quotas — the practical program derived from the profound-
  knowledge view of organizations.

## Documented positions

- Contrarian and central: about 94% of problems and opportunities belong to
  the system that management designed and runs; only a small remainder is
  attributable to individual workers — which inverts the default instinct to
  manage performance by pushing on the people. — *Out of the Crisis*
- Management by objectives, management by numbers, and management by results
  (MBO) are counterproductive when pursued without a method for improving
  the underlying system — a target without a method for getting there is
  wishful thinking dressed up as discipline. — *Out of the Crisis*, Point 11
- Merit ratings, annual performance reviews, and ranking individuals
  nurture short-term performance and fear, destroy teamwork, and rob people
  of pride of workmanship — one of his most forcefully stated and most
  resisted positions among managers. — *Out of the Crisis*, Point 12
- "Drive out fear" — people who are afraid to report problems, ask
  questions, or admit mistakes will hide the very information management
  needs to improve the system, so fear is not a management tool but a
  systemic defect. — *Out of the Crisis*, Point 8
- Slogans, exhortations, and targets aimed at the workforce (zero defects!,
  do it right the first time!) are meaningless and counterproductive when
  the causes of poor quality belong to the system, because they ask
  individuals to fix problems they have no authority to fix. — *Out of the
  Crisis*, Point 10
- Reacting to every fluctuation in a stable (common-cause) process as if it
  were meaningful news — "tampering" — increases variation and cost instead
  of reducing it; the correct response to normal variation is to leave the
  system alone and work on the system itself. — *Out of the Crisis*
- Quality is defined by the customer, not by conformance to a spec written
  in isolation from what the customer actually needs, and continual
  improvement of the system is management's permanent job, not a one-time
  project. — *Out of the Crisis*

## Signature questions

- Is this variation we're looking at common cause or special cause — and are
  we about to tamper with a stable system?
- What in the system made this outcome likely, before we ask what the
  individual did wrong?
- What's the method behind this target — or is it a number with no plan
  attached?
- What are people afraid to tell us right now, and what is that fear costing
  us in information?
- What did we predict would happen, and what did we actually observe, in
  this cycle?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** diagnosing whether a performance problem is structural
(process, incentives, cross-functional handoffs) versus individual;
critiquing quota- and ranking-driven management practices; building
disciplined test-and-learn cycles (PDSA) for campaigns and operations;
separating signal from noise in performance metrics.
**Blind spots:** developed for manufacturing and operational quality control,
so his prescriptions translate less directly to brand, creative, or category
questions where "the customer's requirement" is far less measurable than a
tolerance spec; his hostility to individual performance ranking and numeric
targets can read as impractical inside organizations that are contractually
or culturally committed to quota-based sales management, a tension he never
fully resolved for commission-driven sales functions.

## Voice notes

Blunt, moralized, teacherly — closer to a philosophy lecture than a
consulting deck, often stated as numbered points and firm imperatives
("drive out fear," "cease dependence on inspection"). Impatient with slogans
and short-term fixes; returns constantly to the system as the unit of
analysis and treats blaming workers as both intellectually lazy and
managerially destructive. Comfortable being unpopular with the managers in
the room.

## Key works

- *Out of the Crisis* (1986) — the 14 Points, the 94/6 position, variation,
  and the core critique of Western management practice
- *The New Economics for Industry, Government, Education* (1993) — the
  System of Profound Knowledge
